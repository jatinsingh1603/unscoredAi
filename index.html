Air India Flight AI171 — June 12, 2025 — Ahmedabad

$475 Million in Claims. $20 Billion at Stake. The Question Nobody Asked.

A Boeing 787-8 Dreamliner crashed seconds after takeoff from Ahmedabad, killing 260 people. Estimated insurance claims: $475 million — $125M for the aircraft hull, $350M for passenger and third-party liability. Air India's fleet was insured for $20 billion with TATA AIG, ICICI Lombard, and New India Insurance.

Before those policies were written, how thoroughly did underwriters assess maintenance compliance, operational risk posture, safety records, and cyber systems? Investigations have placed systemic maintenance lapses and regulatory oversight failures under scrutiny. This is not a question about one airline — it is a question about how the entire underwriting process works, and what it fundamentally misses.

Two Cases That Started the Same Way
Mumbai — 2010

Health Insurance Scam

A network of corrupt doctors, hospitals, and agents colluded to defraud insurers. Policyholders underwent unnecessary procedures; falsified bills were submitted. The scam surfaced only when a suspicious senior citizen complained to the regulator — not through any underwriting system.

Delhi — 2017

Auto Insurance Fraud Ring

Stolen vehicles were used in staged accidents to generate fraudulent claims — a sophisticated multi-entity operation. It passed through standard underwriting without a single flag, because the process accepted what it was told and checked nothing independently.
